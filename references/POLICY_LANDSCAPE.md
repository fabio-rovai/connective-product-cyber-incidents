# UK Policy Landscape: Cyber Security of Connective Products

Secondary context for the Connective Product Cyber Incidents Dataset (CPCID). This document traces UK cross-government and parliamentary activity on the cyber security of connective products (consumer IoT, connectable/smart products, operational technology (OT), edge devices, connected infrastructure and software supply chain), 2018 to 2026, with brief international context.

Sourcing rule: only real, verifiable government or parliamentary documents are listed. Items that could not be fully confirmed are marked "unverified" or given an explicit confidence level. Dates and titles were checked against gov.uk, legislation.gov.uk, ncsc.gov.uk, parliament.uk, nao.org.uk, ofgem.gov.uk and official EU/US sources. Compiled 2026-07-01.

## Dated timeline

Ordered by date. Body, activity and a one-line summary for each. Confidence caveats are carried through from the source material.

### 2017

- **~May 2017 (moderate confidence)** POST publishes **POSTnote 554: Cyber Security of UK Infrastructure**, covering OT/ICS in critical national infrastructure (substations, control rooms). <https://post.parliament.uk/research-briefings/post-pn-0554/>

### 2018

- **7 Mar 2018** DCMS (with NCSC) publishes **Secure by Design: Improving the cyber security of consumer Internet of Things** (report), arguing for shifting the security burden off consumers by building security into IoT products by design, and carrying a draft Code of Practice. <https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/secure-by-design-report>
- **10 May 2018** The **Network and Information Systems Regulations 2018 (SI 2018/506)** come into force, the baseline OES/RDSP cyber-resilience regime the later CSR Bill amends. <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2018/506>
- **14 Oct 2018** DCMS (with NCSC) publishes the **Code of Practice for Consumer IoT Security**: 13 outcome-focused guidelines led by (1) no default passwords, (2) implement a vulnerability disclosure policy, (3) keep software updated; aligned to ETSI TS 103 645 / EN 303 645. <https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/code-of-practice-for-consumer-iot-security>

### 2019

- **Feb 2019** POST publishes **POSTnote 593: Cyber Security of Consumer Devices**, precursor evidence to PSTI. <https://post.parliament.uk/research-briefings/post-pn-0593/>
- **1 May to 5 Jun 2019 (consultation); response Jan 2020 (exact day unverified)** DCMS runs the **Consultation on the Government's regulatory proposals regarding consumer IoT security**, proposing to mandate the top three Code guidelines; the response confirmed intent to legislate. <https://www.gov.uk/government/consultations/consultation-on-regulatory-proposals-on-consumer-iot-security>

### 2020 to 2021

- **16 Jul to 6 Sep 2020 (call for views); response 21 Apr 2021** DCMS runs **Proposals for regulating consumer smart product cyber security: call for views**, setting out a mandatory scheme for consumer connectable products on three baseline requirements; the response committed to legislate when parliamentary time allowed. <https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/proposals-for-regulating-consumer-smart-product-cyber-security-call-for-views>
- **15 Dec 2021 (page shows a 15 Dec 2022 reissue)** Cabinet Office publishes the **National Cyber Strategy 2022**, a whole-of-society strategy across five pillars; Pillar 2 cites UK leadership on secure-by-design connectable consumer products and commits to supply-chain risk, and Pillar 3 commits to "secure the next generation of connected technologies" using regulatory levers "as we have done in telecoms". Does not name PSTI explicitly. <https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/national-cyber-strategy-2022>

### 2022

- **25 Jan 2022 (search-level confidence)** Cabinet Office (Government Security Group) publishes the **Government Cyber Security Strategy: 2022 to 2030**, a distinct strategy to harden the public sector's own systems; underpins GovAssure. <https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/government-cyber-security-strategy-2022-to-2030>
- **1 Apr 2022 (updated 14 Jan 2026)** Ofgem publishes **NIS Directive and NIS Regulations 2018: Ofgem guidance for Operators of Essential Services**, OES cyber-security guidance for downstream gas/electricity using an Ofgem-adapted NCSC CAF profile with elevated OT/ICS emphasis. <https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/guidance/nis-directive-and-nis-regulations-2018-ofgem-guidance-operators-essential-services>
- **May 2022** The Drinking Water Inspectorate (with Ofwat funding linkage) issues **Guidance on SEMD and NIS requirements (PR24)**, NIS/SEMD cyber expectations for water companies using annual NCSC CAF returns (an enhanced "e-CAF" standard is expected from 31 Mar 2028).
- **6 Jul 2022 (closed 28 Sep 2022)** BEIS (now DESNZ) runs the smart-energy consultation **Delivering a smart and secure electricity system: interoperability and cyber security of energy smart appliances and remote load control**, proposing minimum cyber-security (ETSI EN 303 645) and grid-stability requirements for energy smart appliances (heat pumps, batteries, EV chargers); basis of the Smart Secure Electricity Systems (SSES) programme. <https://www.gov.uk/government/consultations/delivering-a-smart-and-secure-electricity-system-the-interoperability-and-cyber-security-of-energy-smart-appliances-and-remote-load-control>
- **6 Dec 2022 (Royal Assent)** UK Parliament passes the **Product Security and Telecommunications Infrastructure Act 2022 (c. 46)**; Part 1 empowers ministers to set security requirements for relevant connectable consumer products and imposes duties on manufacturers, importers and distributors, with enforcement powers (compliance/stop/recall notices, monetary penalties). <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/46>

### 2023

- **7 Mar 2018 onward baseline; 14 Sep 2023 (made), in force 29 Apr 2024** DCMS/DSIT make the **PSTI (Security Requirements for Relevant Connectable Products) Regulations 2023 (SI 2023/1007)**, setting the three mandatory requirements (no universal default passwords, published vulnerability disclosure policy, transparency on minimum security-update period) plus a statement of compliance; extends to all four nations. <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2023/1007/contents>
- **13 Dec 2023** The Joint Committee on the National Security Strategy (JCNSS) publishes **A hostage to fortune: ransomware and UK national security** (First Report 2023-24), warning the UK is unprepared for a catastrophic ransomware attack on largely privately-operated, legacy-IT CNI; it criticised Home Office lead and recommended moving responsibility to the Cabinet Office. <https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/jt5804/jtselect/jtnatsec/194/report.html>
- **27 Nov 2023** NCSC with US CISA (and 18 countries) publishes the **Guidelines for secure AI system development**, the first global guidelines for secure AI across four lifecycle stages (secure design, development, deployment, operation/maintenance), secure-by-default. <https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/collection/guidelines-secure-ai-system-development>

### 2024

- **8 Jan 2024 (updated 17 Mar 2025)** OPSS (DBT) and DSIT publish the **business guidance "Regulations: consumer connectable product security"**, the primary compliance guidance for manufacturers, importers and distributors ahead of the regime's commencement. <https://www.gov.uk/guidance/regulations-consumer-connectable-product-security>
- **Feb 2024** CISA, NSA and FBI publish joint advisory **AA24-038a**, the canonical primary source on Volt Typhoon living-off-the-land TTPs against US critical infrastructure (relevant to the OT/CNI strand of the debate). <https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/cybersecurity-advisories/aa24-038a>
- **21 Feb and 24 Apr 2024 (oral evidence; launched 2023)** The Science, Innovation and Technology Committee takes evidence in its inquiry **Cyber resilience of the UK's critical national infrastructure**, examining CNI cyber threats, secure-by-design hardware and 2025 resilience targets. No final report found; inquiry appears to have lapsed at dissolution (report unverified / likely not published). <https://committees.parliament.uk/work/7934/cyber-resilience-of-the-uks-critical-national-infrastructure/>
- **29 Apr 2024** The **PSTI mandatory regime comes into force**, making the three baseline requirements enforceable for relevant connectable consumer products.
- **17 Jul 2024** HM Government announces the **Cyber Security and Resilience Bill** in the King's Speech, to strengthen defences of essential services, infrastructure and digital services. <https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/cyber-security-and-resilience-bill>

### 2025

- **29 Jan 2025** The National Audit Office publishes **Government cyber resilience** (value-for-money report): government will not meet its 2025 goal; approximately 228 significant legacy systems (Mar 2024), half without funded remediation; a severe skills shortage. <https://www.nao.org.uk/reports/government-cyber-resilience/>
- **9 Apr 2025** DSIT publishes the **Cyber Security and Resilience Bill: policy statement**, confirming scope: managed service providers (MSPs) brought in; data centres designated CNI (Sep 2024) with regulatory oversight; a "Designated Critical Suppliers" power; and expansion/strengthening of the NIS Regulations 2018. <https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/cyber-security-and-resilience-bill-policy-statement/cyber-security-and-resilience-bill-policy-statement>
- **9 May 2025** The Committee of Public Accounts publishes **Government cyber resilience** (report HC 643): government will miss its end-2025 cyber-resilience target; resilience lower than estimated; approximately 319 legacy systems flagged (about 25% "red"); one in three cyber roles unfilled; supply-chain risk (for example Synnovis/NHS). <https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm5901/cmselect/cmpubacc/643/report.html>- **6 Aug 2025** NCSC releases **Cyber Assessment Framework (CAF) v4.0** (4 objectives, 14 principles, 39 outcomes) for the cyber resilience of essential functions; applied to NIS operators of essential services, CNI and public bodies under GovAssure. Some NCSC collection-page metadata still shows an older "18 April 2024" date for an earlier release; the authoritative current version is v4.0 and the stale metadata should not be cited. <https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/collection/cyber-assessment-framework>
- **10 Sep 2025** POST publishes **POSTnote 753: Cyber resilience of UK digital infrastructure** (threats, resilient system design and AI-enabled defence). Note this is "digital infrastructure", not OT-specific. <https://post.parliament.uk/research-briefings/post-pn-0753/>
- **12 Nov 2025 (Commons first reading)** The **Cyber Security and Resilience (Network and Information Systems) Bill (Bill 4035)** is introduced in the Commons, amending and expanding the NIS regime. <https://bills.parliament.uk/bills/4035>

### 2026

- **6 Jan 2026 (Commons second reading)** The CSR Bill (Bill 4035) has its Commons second reading; Committee stage runs 3 to 24 Feb 2026, and Report and third reading complete on 10 Jun 2026, after which it moves to the Lords (second reading scheduled 14 Jul 2026). Royal Assent widely expected late 2026; implementation not before approximately 2028. Stage confidence: high that the Bill has passed the Commons and is in the Lords; the 10 Jun 2026 Commons completion date is confirmed at search level. <https://bills.parliament.uk/bills/4035>
- **Jan 2026** DSIT / Government Cyber Unit publishes the **Government Cyber Action Plan** (GBP 210m+), sitting downstream of the NAO/PAC findings.
- **14 Jan 2026** Ofgem updates its **NIS OES guidance** (see 2022 entry) with continued OT/ICS emphasis. <https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/guidance/nis-directive-and-nis-regulations-2018-ofgem-guidance-operators-essential-services>
- **30 Apr 2026** DSIT (with Home Office; fieldwork by Ipsos) publishes the latest **Cyber Security Breaches Survey (2025/2026 edition)**, Official Statistics measuring UK business/charity cyber policies, breach prevalence and impact; the 2025/26 headline was that 43% of businesses and 28% of charities reported a breach/attack in the last 12 months. <https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/cyber-security-breaches-survey-20252026>
- **Not yet published (moderate confidence)** DSIT's statutory **post-implementation review of the PSTI regime**: the review duty sits in Regulation 10 of SI 2023/1007, with the first report due within five years of commencement (i.e. by approximately 29 Apr 2029). No PIR report exists yet as of July 2026. The regime has since been amended (for example motor vehicles excluded via a 2025 amendment). <https://www.gov.uk/guidance/regulations-consumer-connectable-product-security>

### Enforcement (ongoing, OPSS/DBT)

OPSS, part of the Department for Business and Trade, is the PSTI enforcement authority, acting for DSIT under a memorandum of understanding. Its enforcement guidance (first published Jun 2025; updated 8 Aug 2025) sets out powers: compliance/stop/recall notices, forfeiture, and monetary penalties up to the greater of GBP 10m or 4% of qualifying worldwide revenue, plus daily penalties up to GBP 20,000. <https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/opss-enforcement-enforcement-actions/consumer-connectable-product-security-regulations>

Enforcement caveat (unverified): the public pages document powers and process. No published record of a specific named monetary penalty already imposed on a company under PSTI was found as of July 2026. Treat "penalties already issued" as unverified.

### NCSC standing technical guidance (living documents)

Underpinning much departmental policy: **Device security principles for manufacturers / Device security guidance**; the **Organisational use of Enterprise Connected Devices** threat report; **Secure design principles** (five categories: establish context, make compromise difficult, make disruption difficult, make detection easier, reduce impact); **Connected Places Cyber Security Principles** (14 principles, originally May 2020, refreshed via the Secure Connected Places programme); and consumer-facing **Smart devices: using them safely in your home**. The **smart-metering security model** (SMKI / DCC / Smart Energy Code) rests on the DCC central comms hub, SMKI public-key infrastructure and SEC-mandated governance. (Correction to a common misconception: Ofwat is the economic regulator; the NIS competent authority for the water sector is the Secretary of State acting through the DWI/Defra, not Ofwat.)

## International context (brief)

| Instrument | Body | Key dates | Summary |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EU Cyber Resilience Act, Regulation (EU) 2024/2847** | European Parliament & Council | Adopted 23 Oct 2024; in force 10 Dec 2024; vulnerability/incident reporting from 11 Sep 2026; main obligations from 11 Dec 2027 | Horizontal mandatory cybersecurity requirements for "products with digital elements", CE marking and lifecycle vulnerability handling. Broader and more prescriptive than PSTI (all products with digital elements, not just consumer). <https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/2847/oj/eng> |
| **U.S. Cyber Trust Mark (FCC 24-26, PS Docket 23-239)** | Federal Communications Commission | Report and Order adopted 14 Mar 2024; launch announced Jan 2025 | Voluntary cybersecurity labelling programme for consumer IoT (logo plus QR code to a product registry; UL Solutions as lead administrator). A voluntary label, versus the UK's mandatory floor. <https://www.fcc.gov/CyberTrustMark> |
| **NIST IR 8425: Profile of the IoT Core Baseline for Consumer IoT Products** | NIST | 20 Sep 2022 | Technical consumer-IoT baseline underpinning the FCC trust mark. <https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/ir/8425/final> |

The UK sits between the EU (mandatory, broad, CE-marked) and the US (voluntary label). The UK's PSTI is mandatory but narrow (consumer connectable products, three baseline controls); the CSR Bill extends the mandatory perimeter through the NIS/services route rather than the product route.

## Synthesis: the cross-department evidence gap

Two through-lines run under all of these documents. First, responsibility for the security of a connected device is diffuse and tends to sit with the wrong party: the consumer cannot secure the device (Secure by Design, 2018), the operator inherits OT risk it did not design (Ofgem/DWI CAF regimes), the buyer of a managed service cannot see the supply chain (CSR Bill's Designated Critical Suppliers power), and government itself cannot secure its own estate (NAO/PAC, 2025). Every regime pushes the duty back up the chain to whoever controls the design, the update and the vulnerability-disclosure process. Secure-by-design and vulnerability disclosure are the two controls that appear in almost every document from 2018 to 2026. Second, scope creep by necessity: policy started with consumer IoT (narrow, three controls), then extended to enterprise-connected devices, operational technology in energy and water (SSES, Ofgem/DWI CAF), managed service providers and critical suppliers (CSR Bill), and the government's own connected estate. The perimeter keeps widening because attackers move to whatever is left unregulated.

The single clearest cross-department evidence gap is this: there is no published, quantified evidence base on the security of enterprise and operational-technology connective products (OT/ICS, edge devices, industrial and enterprise IoT). Rich, repeatable evidence exists only for the consumer and the enterprise-IT ends:

- The Cyber Security Breaches Survey (annual since 2016) measures organisational cyber posture and IT breaches, not product-level or OT device security.
- The NAO/PAC work quantifies government legacy IT and skills gaps, not connected-product or OT exposure.
- PSTI's own evidence base is the 2018 to 2021 consultation cycle plus POSTnote 593 (2019); **no post-implementation review has been published** (due approximately 2029), so there is no measured evidence yet that the mandatory regime has reduced insecure devices in market.
- OT/ICS in CNI is covered by CAF self-assessment returns (Ofgem, DWI) that are not published as an aggregate evidence base, and by ageing POST material (POSTnote 554, 2017). POSTnote 753 (2025) is explicitly digital-infrastructure, not OT.

In short: government legislates confidently on consumer devices where it has data, and legislates on OT and enterprise connective products where it largely does not. That absence of a quantified OT/enterprise-product evidence base is the standing gap, and it is the same gap the CPCID incident dataset works toward, at the incident level, by assembling a curated, product-class-indexed, provenance-graded corpus of documented incidents across all five technology groups.
